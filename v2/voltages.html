Why grid voltages are so high

A 1 GW transmission line carries about the same power as 100,000 UK homes at once. Do that at domestic voltage and you'd need a copper cable the thickness of a tree trunk — and you'd still lose most of the energy as heat before it arrived. So transmission goes high, and distribution fans it out.

The physics: losses scale with current squared

Moving power P through a wire of resistance R dissipates heat:

P_loss = I² × R   where   I = P / (√3 × V)  (three-phase)

So for a fixed power, doubling voltage halves the current — and quarters the losses. 400 kV is ~36× higher than 11 kV, so losses are ~1300× lower. That's why GB transmission loses only ~1.7 % over hundreds of km, and why you'd never distribute at the generator's own 20 kV.

The fan-out: high voltage splits into many low-voltage paths

One 1 GW generator feeds millions of customers. The network branches at every voltage step — transmission (400/275/132 kV) fans into primary distribution (typically 132 → 33 → 11 kV, though voltages vary by area — some secondaries run at 6.6 kV) which fans into low voltage (400 V) which finally reaches the socket. Each tier trades voltage for branch count, because safety and insulation cost rise with voltage while branching cost falls. The orange pulses are power flowing outward.
